Research

In my research, I aim to bridge bioinformatics approaches—including phylogenomics and computational structural biology—to generate hypotheses and ask new questions. By integrating insights from structural biology, biochemistry, and evolutionary analysis, we aim to capture the big picture while keeping the finer details in focus. The main facets of my work can be summarized as:

1. Phylogenomics of Plant Immune Receptors

Plants have a variety of immune receptors that detect pathogen invasions and provide immunity. These receptors can be located on the cell surface, such as leucine-rich repeat receptor kinases and proteins (RLKs and RLPs, PRRs), or within the cytoplasm, like nucleotide-binding leucine-rich repeat receptors (NLRs). My research focuses on exploring the diversity of plant immune receptors and other immunity-associated genes at both inter and intraspecies levels [1–4].

2. Molecular Evolution of Plant Immune Receptors and Pathogen Effectors

With the massive expansion and diversity of plant immune receptors we often look into the evolutionary history of gene families to understand how plants and pathogens co-evolve with each other. My research focuses on understanding the differential evolutionary pace of immune receptor types and families [3,5,6].

3. Computational Structural Biology

Recent advances in structural modeling have enabled high-throughput computational structural analysis of plant immune receptors and pathogen effectors. My research focuses on studying the structural basis of the resting and activated states of plant NLRs, PRRs, and structural diversity of pathogen effectors [6–8].
